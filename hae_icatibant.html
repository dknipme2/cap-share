SAJAZIR (Icatibant) — HAE On-Demand

SAJAZIR (icatibant) is a synthetic decapeptide bradykinin B2 receptor antagonist, delivered as a subcutaneous self-administered injection for acute HAE attacks in adults 18+. Marketed as an ANDA generic by Cycle Pharmaceuticals after the original Firazyr brand lost exclusivity in July 2019; multiple generics now compete. The commercial surface is Cycle Vita hub services and Orsini specialty-pharmacy training, not clinical differentiation.

Shifting Treatment Landscape

As of mid-2025, the HAE on-demand segment faces direct displacement pressure from Ekterly (sebetralstat) — the first oral on-demand therapy. SAJAZIR (and the icatibant class broadly) retains clinical legitimacy (proven B2 antagonist mechanism, sub-2-hour onset, laryngeal-attack experience) but loses on patient-experience dimensions: no injection, no needle anxiety, approved down to age 12. Analytics for SAJAZIR should focus on the retention pocket (intolerance, failure, access-restricted patients) rather than growth.

Disease & Epidemiology

Hereditary angioedema is an autosomal dominant disorder of the complement / kinin pathway. In type I (80–85% of cases), the SERPING1 gene encodes deficient C1-esterase inhibitor (C1-INH). In type II (15–20%), C1-INH levels are normal or elevated but the protein is dysfunctional. In type III (HAE-nl-C1INH), C1-INH is normal and mutations in F12, ANGPT1, PLG, or other genes drive pathology — often estrogen-sensitive, often female. Deficient C1-INH allows unchecked kallikrein activity, bradykinin overproduction, and episodic vascular permeability — swelling of extremities, face, GI tract, and larynx.

Clinical hallmarks

  • Recurrent angioedema attacks — swelling of extremities, face, or GI tract lasting hours to days
  • Laryngeal attacks are life-threatening; 36.9% of HAE-C1INH patients report family members who died from an HAE attack
  • Non-pitting, non-pruritic — distinguishes from histamine-mediated angioedema
  • Abdominal attacks can mimic acute abdomen — multiple patients have had exploratory laparotomies before diagnosis
  • Triggers: trauma, dental procedures, infections, stress, estrogen, ACE inhibitors
  • Attack frequency varies — some patients weekly, others yearly; unpredictable

Patient Journey

Childhood / adolescence
First attacks — often unrecognized
First angioedema attacks typically begin in late childhood or adolescence. Triggers may not be obvious. Family history is often present but not always disclosed or elicited. Many patients are dismissed as having allergic reactions and trialed on antihistamines or steroids (which do not work for bradykinin-mediated angioedema).
Decades of misdiagnosis
Recurrent attacks attributed to allergy, anxiety, or idiopathic angioedema
Patients may see PCPs, allergists, gastroenterologists (for abdominal attacks mimicking acute abdomen), dermatologists, and ER physicians. Repeated courses of corticosteroids, epinephrine, and antihistamines fail to prevent or abort attacks — because none of these treat the bradykinin pathway. Some patients undergo exploratory laparotomies for what was really an HAE abdominal attack.
Allergy/immunology referral
Specialist workup — C4 is the gateway test
A specialist (allergist/immunologist) with HAE awareness orders C4 as the screening test. Low C4 between attacks is highly suggestive. C1-INH antigen and C1-INH functional assays follow. Genetic testing for SERPING1 confirms type I/II; type III testing is broader (F12, PLG, ANGPT1, etc.).
Diagnosis
Treatment plan: on-demand + (usually) prophylaxis
Modern HAE care pairs an on-demand rescue therapy (icatibant — SAJAZIR (Cycle), Firazyr (Takeda), or other generics — C1-INH concentrates, or now oral sebetralstat) with long-term prophylaxis (Takhzyro, Orladeyo, or newer entrants). Patients are trained on self-injection for on-demand treatment. Action plans for laryngeal attacks (auto-injector carry, ER identification cards) are established.
Ongoing care
Attack plan, prophylaxis adjustment, advocacy integration
Patients typically engage with the HAEA (US Hereditary Angioedema Association), register with the HAEA Scientific Registry, and are managed long-term by an HAE-literate allergy/immunology specialist. Prophylaxis choice is revisited as new options arrive (Orladeyo 2020, Takhzyro pediatric 2023, Andembry 2025, Dawnzera 2025).

Who orders testing?

  • Allergists / immunologists — primary prescriber universe (~4,000–5,000 board-certified in US, with a smaller HAE-literate subset)
  • HAE Centers of Excellence — academic programs with dedicated HAE clinics (Penn State ICE-HAE, USHAEA affiliates, GA2LEN ACARE network)
  • Emergency medicine — acute attacks present to ED; HAE-aware EPs can trigger workup
  • Medical geneticists — increasingly involved in SERPING1 testing
  • PCPs — can order C4 as first step but referral to allergy/immunology is typical

Claims data is adequate for defensive analytics — retention analysis, switching patterns to oral prophylaxis, payer-specific on-demand access mapping. But the market trajectory is downward as oral prophylaxis and oral on-demand erode injectable on-demand use. Analysis confirms the decline and optimizes defensive positioning; it does not reverse the direction. Frame ROI accordingly: this is a portfolio-defense workstream, not a growth workstream.

Claims-Based Identification Opportunities

Unlike alkaptonuria (no specific ICD) or periodic paralysis (under-coded), HAE has a usable specific ICD (D84.1) and dedicated billing codes for the major therapeutics. Claims-based identification is the primary substrate, supplemented by allergy / immunology visit patterns and ED-utilization signals.

1 · Structured Claims Signal

D84.1 + treatment-specific claims are the canonical HAE identification algorithm. Enrich with attack coding for higher specificity.

  • Primary ICD: D84.1 (defects in the complement system, including HAE subcodes)
  • Attack context: T78.3XXA / T78.3XXD (angioneurotic edema)
  • HCPCS J-codes: J1744 (icatibant), J0596 (C1-INH concentrate), J0597, J0598, J1290 (ecallantide), J0599 (lanadelumab)
  • NDCs for oral prophylaxis (Orladeyo) and oral on-demand (Ekterly) once claims mature

Expected yield: Approximately 8,000–10,000 patients identifiable through combined algorithms. Claims-based prevalence studies converge on this range.

2 · Rx Pattern Signals — On-Demand + Prophylaxis Overlap

Modern HAE patients typically carry BOTH an on-demand therapy AND a long-term prophylaxis. The overlap pattern is diagnostic and identifies treatment-shift patterns.

  • Chronic dispense of injectable on-demand (icatibant, C1-INH)
  • Chronic dispense of oral prophylaxis (Orladeyo) or SC prophylaxis (Takhzyro, Andembry, Dawnzera)
  • Co-occurrence identifies "dual-therapy" patients (the dominant modern HAE phenotype)
  • Switching patterns: injectable on-demand → oral on-demand (Ekterly) transition tracking

3 · Allergy/Immunology Visit Frequency

Repeated allergy/immunology visits in a young adult, especially female, with recurrent angioedema codes is a diagnostic signature.

  • ≥3 allergy/immunology visits within 12 months with angioedema ICD
  • Patient age 15–50, female predominant
  • Family history of angioedema (structured or narrative)
  • Referral from ER → allergy/immunology

4 · ED Utilization Signals

Pre-diagnosis HAE patients present to the ED repeatedly with angioedema, abdominal pain, or airway symptoms. Unexplained ED utilization is a patient-finding signal.

  • ED visits for angioedema (T78.3XXA) in young adults
  • Repeated abdominal-pain ED visits without alternative diagnosis
  • ED visits with laryngeal edema, particularly recurrent
  • Corticosteroid / epinephrine / antihistamine treatment without durable response

5 · Negative-Workup Signals

HAE patients carry the distinctive signature of an angioedema workup that rules OUT allergic causes. Negative-test patterns are identification leads.

  • Negative IgE panels, negative tryptase, negative skin-prick testing
  • C4 ordered (or notably NOT ordered despite recurrent angioedema)
  • Multiple courses of H1 / H2 antihistamines without response

6 · Genetic-Panel Order Signals

SERPING1 sequencing orders, HAE gene panels, and C1-INH functional assays at reference labs are the highest-precision diagnosed-but-unreached signals.

  • Commercial lab HAE panel orders (SERPING1, F12, PLG)
  • C1-INH functional assay results at reference labs
  • Positive-result patients not yet on therapy (diagnostic delay)

Open Questions

  1. Ekterly (oral on-demand) uptake in the first 12–18 months post-launch — what percentage of injectable on-demand patients convert?
  2. Intolerance / failure cohort size — how many oral-prophylaxis patients experience breakthrough attacks requiring injectable on-demand?
  3. Payer step-therapy posture — do payers restrict injectable on-demand in favor of oral, and which payers move first?
  4. Pediatric market dynamics — Ekterly is 12+; does the <12 injectable market persist, and for how long?
  5. Orsini Specialty Pharmacy data-reporting status to commercial Rx panels — typical hub-driven rare-disease data-visibility questions apply; Cycle Vita hub telemetry is the fallback if Orsini doesn't report
  6. Laryngeal-attack-history segment — do clinicians retain injectable preference for this high-risk subgroup?
  7. WAC pricing of oral on-demand (Ekterly) vs. injectable on-demand — price-sensitive payers may drive injectable preservation if oral is priced aggressively
