Ch. 04Day 10 — the CCO reviews

The dashboard is the conversation

The CCO opens the launch-readiness board in claude.ai before the Monday prep. The sizing number is there — 47. The board also shows target HCPs, access tier, workstream readiness, the five readiness stripes. And a cursor.

The CCO types: why 47 and not 52?

The answer comes back layered. The first layer is data-driven: target universe refined down 8% after the decile update, call-productivity benchmark revised up 6% based on last quarter's response analysis, marginal-rep productivity drops sharply past rep 47. Under each sentence, a trace block: which finding, which method, which input. Every number has its receipt one click away.

There is a quieter second layer the CCO can expand under expert input integrated. The decile model isn't just quantitative. Last quarter's territory roundtables flagged top-decile accounts as over-indexed relative to actual response lift — and that field-force feedback was captured as typed findings in the graph at the time, validated against silver-layer prescriptions, and is now feeding the decile model directly. Tacit knowledge made explicit. Saved once. Referenced every time.

The recommendation isn't "the model said 47." It is "the model said 47 because the data moved, the reps told us something the data didn't, and here's the audit trail for both." Data-driven, expert-augmented, auditable. That is the differentiator that makes 47 defensible not just in this meeting, but in any meeting six months from now when someone re-litigates the call.

The CCO asks the follow-up: what's the revenue risk at 47 vs 52? Peak-year revenue at 47 lands ~$220M with 88% confidence. At 52, peak rises ~$8M — but the incremental reps sit on the flat part of the curve. The 47 sizing is the knee; going higher mostly adds cost.

Decision captured. Lock 47. Show me the territory design. The next view pulls from the same graph. Same IDs. Same method. Same evidence trail.

Ch. 05Day 14 — the board meeting

One page of reasoning. Two questions of depth at the ready.

The CCO presents the sizing recommendation to the board. The number is 47. The one page behind it shows: method, data sources, sensitivity, risks considered, defensive-lever inventory if actuals diverge from plan. Every line has a citation.

A director asks how the revenue risk compares to peer launches at similar stage. The CCO opens claude.ai on the spot. Show me peer launches in this indication at T+6 months post-launch. The peer-asset registry returns four analog assets with trajectory splines. Comparison delivered in under a minute.

The recommendation is approved. The decision, the supporting findings, and the peer-benchmark query all persist back to the graph. This is the starting state of the next commercial question at this company.

Ch. 07The implication

Your commercial intelligence is now an asset you own

This is the line that matters for the CCO, the CFO, the board. Not "AI is faster." Not "analysts are cheaper." The line is about what the company owns at the end.

At the end of a ninety-day (10–13 week) consulting engagement, the company owned a deck. Maybe a model file. A trail of emails. The reasoning lived in the consultants' heads and left when they did.

At the end of fourteen days of CAP-supported analysis, the company owns a typed graph of decisions, findings, methodologies, and evidence. The next analyst — whether in-house, consulting, or embedded — starts from that graph. The company's analytical IP is finally on its own balance sheet.

This is the compounding asset. Every engagement contributes. Every friction becomes a rule. The capability gets cheaper — and sharper — the more the company uses it. That is the go-to-market case.
