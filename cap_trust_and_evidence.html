CAP · Trust & evidence

How do I know I can trust what the AI gives me?

Every factual claim is flagged. Every brief carries a validation score. Every recommendation traces back through the graph to a named source. Unflagged text is assumed unverified; only [CONFIRMED] claims are treated as facts in deliverables. The mechanism is the same one an analyst would use — made durable and auditable by the system.

The starting posture

Three kinds of answers, three kinds of trust

Not all AI output deserves the same trust. The system classifies every claim on the way out so the reader knows which is which, before acting.

Trust · verified

Confirmed facts

Human-verified or primary-source-verified claims. Tagged [CONFIRMED: source]. These land in the deliverable as facts. Everything else does not.

Use · with context

Structural & directional

Mechanisms, patterns, ranges, trends. Tagged [STRUCTURAL], [DIRECTIONAL], or [EXISTS]. Safe to reason with; surface the flag alongside.

Verify · don't quote

Specific, calculable, stale

Exact numbers from the LLM, things that should be computed from data, or facts that may have aged. Tagged [SPECIFIC], [CALCULABLE], [STALE: date]. Don't quote these — replace with computation or re-verify.

The rule behind the flags

All unflagged claims are [ESTIMATED] — LLM-generated, not independently verified. Treat as directional until flagged otherwise. Only [CONFIRMED] claims are treated as facts in deliverables.

Flags are sticky: every consumer downstream (card, methodology module, engagement delivery) can suppress a flag at the viewing layer, but never strip it from the data layer. The audit trail stays intact all the way to the reader.

The brief-level validation score

One number that summarizes "can I use this?"

Every research brief carries a validation score in frontmatter. It's the share of claims that are either verified, existence-confirmed, or structural. Above 0.80 is green; below 0.50 needs review before the brief lands anywhere.

Formula
validation_score =
confirmed + exists + structural
total_claims
Rating bands
> 0.80 GREEN Safe for engagement use
0.50 – 0.80 YELLOW Usable with caution; surface flagged items
< 0.50 RED Too many unverified — needs review before use

The Cycle engagement's current active brief sits at validation_score: 0.97 — published only after two rounds of adversarial counter-validation against fresh-context subagents.

The research flow

From question to flagged brief to scored artifact

Every research question runs through the same six-step pipe. The KB is checked before any subagent fires; every brief lands with a validation score and a full source list.

KB retrieval · why the answers are grounded

Hybrid retrieval with a cross-encoder rerank

When the system answers from the KB, it isn't guessing from the model's memory. Three retrieval stages compose, and the rerank stage is load-bearing — removing it demonstrably degrades evaluation accuracy.

STAGE 01 · BM25

Keyword match

Classical sparse retrieval against the KB corpus. Fast, exact, good at finding the specific term. Run via tools/kb_index.py.

STAGE 02 · Vector

Semantic similarity

Dense embeddings for conceptually-adjacent matches the keyword layer misses. Merged with BM25 via reciprocal rank fusion.

STAGE 03 · Rerank

Cross-encoder judgment

A cross-encoder re-scores the top candidates. The load-bearing stage — the eval harness .groundskeeper/eval_30q.py shows disabling rerank underperforms vector-only. Never disabled.

The audit chain

From claim to source — every link queryable

When a manager challenges an answer, the system traces the chain back. Every link is a structured object with a stable ID; the trail doesn't disappear when the conversation ends.

CLAIM → An inline assertion in an answer or brief. Carries a confidence flag. Example: FF sizing ≈ 47 reps [CONFIRMED: method workload-buildup-v3.2].
FLAG + SOURCE → The flag names the verification layer. The source is a cited URL, document, dataset, or prior finding entity. Cited inline; never detachable.
RB BRIEF → A structured research brief at knowledge/research/RB_*.md. Carries frontmatter validation score, full claims breakdown, counter-validation results.
ENTITY → A typed graph entity (finding, recommendation, assumption, risk...) with stable ID. Cross-referenced via entity_id and entity_mentions fields. Queryable via tools/entity_graph.py.
DECISION → A decision record tying the recommendation to the outcome. Persists across engagements. Becomes the starting context for the next time the same question surfaces.

The honest limits

What the system does not claim

It doesn't eliminate hallucination. LLMs still make up facts. What the system does is force every claim into a category that exposes whether it was verified — so hallucinated claims land as [SPECIFIC] or [ESTIMATED], not as facts. The flag is the defense.

Validation score isn't truth. A 0.97 score means 97% of claims carry strong confidence flags — not that 97% are true. Counter-validation catches obvious hallucination; deliberate deception or systematic gaps in the source corpus still pass through. Surface-level numeric trust isn't a substitute for the analyst's read.

The chain depends on discipline. Flags only work if they're applied. Cards work only if they link to briefs. Entities work only if they carry stable IDs. Where the discipline breaks down — a brief with no counter-validation, a card with no validation_score — the next consumer should notice and push back. That's what the role-specific handling rules exist for.
