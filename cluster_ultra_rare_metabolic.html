Ultra-Rare Metabolic Cluster — HARLIKU + NITYR + JAVYGTOR

Cycle Pharmaceuticals' three ultra-rare inborn-errors-of-metabolism products — HARLIKU (nitisinone for AKU), NITYR (nitisinone tablets for HT-1), and JAVYGTOR (sapropterin for PKU) — share a single fact that drives the entire analytical case: the same ~150–250 metabolic geneticists, in the same ~100–150 centers of excellence, prescribe all three through the Cycle Vita hub and LeMed specialty pharmacy, to patient populations that often live in overlapping geographies. Public data shows almost none of this. Cycle Vita hub data shows all of it. This is Cycle's strongest case for hub-data investment.

The strategic thesis, in one paragraph

Metabolic geneticists who manage one rare inborn error of metabolism almost always manage others. The same center that starts NITYR on an HT-1 neonate likely prescribes JAVYGTOR for BH4-responsive PKU in the same patient's cousins, and — if the center has a mature adult-transition program — HARLIKU in a middle-aged AKU patient who spent two decades in rheumatology before diagnosis. Three Cycle products, one prescriber universe, one Cycle Vita hub, overlapping geographies, shared adherence dynamics. The traditional siloed rep-per-brand structure makes this invisible to Cycle's ~29 US reps. Portfolio-penetration analytics — “which centers prescribe one of HARLIKU / NITYR / JAVYGTOR but not the others” — require Cycle Vita hub data, and they are the single highest-leverage analytical asset Cycle can build.

AKU is the asymmetric member

JAVYGTOR (PKU) and NITYR (HT-1) are NBS-diagnosed at birth — the commercial challenge is retention. HARLIKU (AKU) is a different disease shape: adult-onset, undiagnosed in 80–95% of the theoretical population, and a patient-finding problem dominated by EHR-NLP and genotype-lab analytics, not hub optimization. Cycle's cluster analytical playbook must treat HARLIKU as distinct on the finding axis while grouping it with NITYR / JAVYGTOR on the prescriber / hub / adherence axes. Don't average the three — model them as parallel tracks converging on the same metabolic center.

Strategic Framing — Why Cluster, Not Per-Product

A per-product analytical approach treats each Cycle rare-disease franchise as an independent business. It misses the single fact that explains the portfolio's commercial shape: one prescriber, three products (HARLIKU, NITYR, JAVYGTOR), partially overlapping patients, shared adherence dynamics. The per-product view makes Cycle's ~29 reps call on the same center three times for three products. The cluster view tells you which center to call on for which product, in what order, based on its actual prescribing pattern across the cluster.

What the per-product view shows

  • HARLIKU brand share — always close to 100% (first approved AKU therapy, no direct competitor)
  • NITYR share of nitisinone molecule — a formulation-preference story inside a ~$100–140M global market vs Orfadin capsules
  • JAVYGTOR share of BH4-responsive PKU — a decline curve against branded Kuvan (slow) and Sephience sepiapterin (fast)
  • Each product's Cycle Vita hub KPIs — PA rate, time-to-first-fill, refill continuity

These are useful. They are also what you get without hub-data integration. They describe each product's performance in isolation and do not answer any portfolio question.

What only the cluster view shows

  • Portfolio penetration — of the ~100–150 US metabolic centers, how many prescribe all three Cycle products (HARLIKU + NITYR + JAVYGTOR), two of three, one, or zero?
  • Cross-sell gaps — centers prescribing NITYR but not HARLIKU (same molecule, same metabolic expertise) are the highest-yield cross-sell targets in Cycle's portfolio
  • Shared transition risk — patients aging out of pediatric JAVYGTOR (PKU) programs are often in the same institutions that treat NITYR (HT-1) children; a single transition-support intervention benefits both products
  • Prescriber-level adherence benchmark — a center's JAVYGTOR adherence performance predicts its NITYR performance, letting weak centers be identified before they produce attrition
  • Field-force prioritization by portfolio weight — one call list ranked by combined portfolio exposure, not three lists ranked by each product separately

Bottom line

Individual products have a weak claims substrate — HARLIKU is effectively invisible, NITYR and JAVYGTOR are specific but pediatric-skewed and PAP-distorted. The strategic cross-sell analytic — “which metabolic centers prescribe one Cycle product but not the others” — is impossible on claims alone because (a) HARLIKU/AKU cannot be cohorted, (b) ultra-rare Part D suppression masks prescriber-level activity, and (c) PAP retention volumes are invisible. Cycle Vita hub data is the unlock. Until hub access is contracted internally, cluster-level analytics are descriptive; with hub access, the cluster becomes the highest-leverage analytical play in Cycle's portfolio.

Overlap estimate — a working hypothesis

No published source enumerates center-level portfolio penetration across HARLIKU + NITYR + JAVYGTOR. Based on the structure of US metabolic genetics — roughly 100–150 centers, heavily overlapping pediatric PKU and HT-1 coverage, far narrower AKU expertise — a working estimate is: ~80–90% of centers prescribe at least one Cycle cluster product, ~50–70% prescribe two, and <20% prescribe all three today. The ~30–50% of two-product centers that are missing the third Cycle product are the primary cross-sell target universe. Validating this estimate requires Cycle Vita hub enrollment data segmented by prescriber NPI and product — a single query, once the data is accessible.

The flywheel, shared across three products

FIND

Identifying the eligible patient.

  • JAVYGTOR (PKU): NBS — essentially universal
  • NITYR (HT-1): NBS — essentially universal
  • HARLIKU (AKU): EHR-NLP + rheumatology / orthopedic pathology note mining + genotype labs — the active problem

ACCESS

Insurance authorization and payer alignment.

  • PA universal for all three
  • Metabolic-specialist consult required by most payers
  • BH4 responsiveness documentation required for sapropterin
  • Hub access specialists handle PA workflow — measurable throughput KPI

START

Time from prescription to first fill.

  • Bridge supply during PA in all three Cycle products
  • Dietary counseling setup for NITYR (HT-1) and JAVYGTOR (PKU)
  • BH4 challenge scheduling for JAVYGTOR (PKU)
  • Slit-lamp baseline + monitoring setup for HARLIKU / NITYR (nitisinone)

MAINTAIN

Ongoing adherence and retention.

  • Refill continuity — the universal adherence metric
  • Dietary adherence (NITYR & JAVYGTOR) — proxied by plasma Phe or Tyr when available
  • Transition support (pediatric → adult for NITYR & JAVYGTOR)
  • Pregnancy-planning support (maternal PKU on JAVYGTOR)

MEASURE

Outcome benchmarking.

  • Plasma Phe (JAVYGTOR / PKU) — ACMG target ≤360 μmol/L lifelong
  • Plasma Tyr (NITYR / HT-1) — target <500 μmol/L
  • Urine HGA (HARLIKU / AKU) — 95–99% reduction on therapy
  • Cross-product: adherence curves + persistence

Shared dietitian & KOL relationships

The non-obvious lever.

  • Metabolic dietitians are the JAVYGTOR (PKU) + NITYR (HT-1) adherence drivers
  • The same dietitians cover both diseases at most centers — single Cycle Vita touchpoint
  • Dietitian education and resources are a cross-product investment with triple leverage across HARLIKU + NITYR + JAVYGTOR
  • NPKUA + AKU Society + pediatric-metabolic advocacy groups — overlap in funding, membership, and conferences

The cluster doesn't need Tier 3 infrastructure to start producing value. Start with the Tier-1 deliverables; stage Tier 2 and Tier 3 on proven value, not speculative scope.

The Big Play

This cluster is the strongest case for Cycle Vita hub-data investment in the portfolio

Every Cycle product has a case for Cycle Vita hub-data. The HARLIKU + NITYR + JAVYGTOR cluster is the case.

Three products, same Cycle Vita hub, same prescribers, overlapping geographies, shared adherence dynamics, and a generational-scale retention challenge (NBS-diagnosed pediatric patients following the disease for 60+ years). No other cluster in Cycle's portfolio has this combination of: (1) near-complete population overlap of the prescriber universe, (2) a shared Cycle Vita hub already in place, (3) a concrete cross-sell gap (centers missing one of three Cycle products), and (4) a shared structural weakness (pediatric-to-adult transition) where a single analytical substrate unlocks interventions for NITYR and JAVYGTOR simultaneously.

Other Cycle portfolio clusters make independent cases for hub-data integration. This one makes the strategic case: the Cycle Vita hub-data investment pays for itself on the metabolic cluster alone, even before portfolio-wide extensions.

What this cluster justifies

  • Cycle Vita hub-data aggregator contract — vendor selection, BAA structure, data-flow architecture (the foundation that every other Cycle hub-distributed product reuses).
  • Tier-1 de-identified substrate on shared compute — the HARLIKU/NITYR/JAVYGTOR portfolio-penetration map and cluster KPI dashboards as the first-year deliverables.
  • Prescriber-NPI-normalized view across all three Cycle products — the single data artifact that makes cross-sell visible.
  • Transition-window alert system — patient-level (de-identified) flag for age-18 boundary refill continuity, shared between NITYR (HT-1) and JAVYGTOR (PKU).
  • Dietitian-education investment line — one Cycle Vita investment, three-product leverage.
  • HARLIKU (AKU) identification pilot scoped inside cluster infrastructure — same metabolic centers, lowest incremental cost.

Open Questions

  1. Hub extractability. Is patient-level hub enrollment and dispense data extractable in a de-identified form suitable for analytics across all three products, and at what granularity (monthly aggregate, event-level, or continuous)?
  2. Patient authorization language. Do existing hub enrollment / patient-authorization forms already name the manufacturer as a data recipient for analytics purposes? If not, what is the re-consent path and timeline?
  3. Center-list completeness. Is there a canonical US metabolic-genetics center list (GMDI / SIMD / HRSA Regional Collaboratives) that can seed the portfolio-penetration denominator — and how complete is it for adult metabolic centers specifically?
  4. Prescriber NPI normalization across products. Are prescribers keyed consistently across the hub's three product lines, or is there NPI-normalization work required before cross-product joins are possible?
  5. Lab-integration footprint. Which metabolic centers share plasma Phe / Tyr / HGA lab values with the hub today, and what is the path to expanding that integration?
  6. Adult-center map. How many US adult metabolic clinics actively accept HT-1 or PKU handoffs, and where are the geographic gaps?
  7. AKU rule-out workup volume inside cluster centers. How many HGA / urine organic acid / HGD gene tests are ordered per year at the ~50 centers that are most likely to identify AKU patients — a signal that feeds the disease-awareness ROI model?
  8. Sepiapterin substitution at cluster centers. Are the centers most loyal to sapropterin also the centers with the strongest portfolio penetration on HT-1 / AKU? If so, cluster-level hub stickiness becomes even more important as a PKU defensive lever.
