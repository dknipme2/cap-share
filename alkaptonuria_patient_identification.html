HARLIKU (Nitisinone) — Alkaptonuria Patient Journey & EHR Identification

Alkaptonuria (AKU) is an ultra-rare metabolic disorder with ~300–1,300 theoretical US patients and fewer than 100 confirmed in registries. The shared ICD-10 code (E70.29) is not AKU-specific, so claims data cannot find these patients. EHR data — structured phenotype plus free-text mining — is the primary route to closing the diagnostic gap. HARLIKU (nitisinone 2 mg) from Cycle Pharmaceuticals is the first and only FDA-approved therapy for adult AKU.

Disease & Epidemiology

Alkaptonuria is an autosomal recessive inborn error of metabolism. Mutations in the HGD gene cause deficient homogentisate 1,2-dioxygenase, so homogentisic acid (HGA) accumulates and polymerizes into a melanin-like pigment that deposits in connective tissue — a process called ochronosis. Over decades, ochronosis drives severe early-onset osteoarthritis, aortic valve disease, and kidney/prostate stones.

Clinical hallmarks

  • Dark urine — turns black on standing/air exposure (pathognomonic, often present from infancy but missed)
  • Ochronotic pigmentation — bluish-black ear cartilage, scleral pigmentation, cerumen discoloration
  • Severe early-onset osteoarthritis — spine, hips, knees, shoulders; atypical pattern for primary OA
  • Aortic stenosis in adults under 60 without usual risk factors
  • Tendon rupture, kidney/prostate stones (often dark-pigmented)
  • Black cartilage noted during joint-replacement surgery — classic incidental finding

Bottom line

Claims data alone cannot find AKU patients. The shared E70.29 bucket produces both low sensitivity and low specificity, and ultra-rare suppression erases what little signal remains. EHR free-text mining, HGD genotype lists from commercial labs, and targeted academic-center phenotype pulls are the actual patient-finding surface. This is the structural reason AKU sits in Tier 2/3 for public-data-only analytics — the honest answer is “buy EHR access” or “fund a disease-awareness intervention,” not “run a claims pull.”

Patient Journey

AKU patients follow a long, fragmented path to diagnosis. Most are symptomatic in their 30s but unrecognized until ochronotic complications force a diagnostic workup — frequently through a surgical or cardiology finding rather than a targeted rule-out.

Infancy / childhood
Dark urine noted — often dismissed or missed
Diaper discoloration, urine darkening on standing. Parents may note but pediatrician rarely orders urinary organic acids. Diagnostic window lost.
Age 20–30
Asymptomatic or minor findings
Pigmented cerumen or scleral speckling may appear but don't trigger workup. Disease is biochemically active — HGA depositing silently into cartilage and connective tissue.
Age 30–40
Joint pain begins (spine > hips > knees)
Primary care → rheumatology. Workup for inflammatory/autoimmune arthritis is uniformly negative (normal RF, CCP, ANA, HLA-B27). Diagnosis frequently delayed for years as “early-onset primary OA.”
Age 40–55
Severe OA → orthopedic surgery
Joint replacement in unusually young patients. Surgeon discovers black cartilage intraoperatively — the strongest single diagnostic cue. Pathology report may mention “ochronotic pigmentation.” Often the first clue that triggers HGA testing.
Age 45–65
Aortic stenosis, renal/prostate stones
Cardiology identifies unexplained aortic valve calcification in a patient without usual risk factors. Urology finds pigmented stones. Any of these can be the trigger for metabolic workup.
Diagnosis
Urine HGA confirmation → genetics
24-hour urine organic acids (GC-MS) reveal massive HGA peak. Molecular genetic testing of HGD confirms biallelic pathogenic variants. Referral to metabolic genetics specialist for management.
On therapy
HARLIKU 2 mg QD via LeMed SP + Cycle Vita hub
Baseline slit-lamp exam; monitor plasma tyrosine, platelets, WBC. HGA drops 95–99% within weeks. Disease progression slows; ochronotic damage is not reversed but arrests.

Who orders testing?

  • Metabolic geneticists — the primary prescriber universe for HARLIKU (likely <50 in the US)
  • Rheumatologists — most common path to referral; primary outreach audience for disease awareness
  • Orthopedic surgeons — discover ochronosis intraoperatively; should be trained to trigger workup on black-cartilage findings
  • Pathologists — identify ochronotic pigmentation in joint specimens; high-leverage education target
  • Urologists — dark-pigmented stones; episodic trigger
  • Cardiothoracic surgery — aortic valve pathology in younger patients

Incidence math

Applying a midpoint prevalence of 1:500,000 to ~335M US population yields an expected point prevalence of ~670 patients. Against ~3.6M US births/year and an autosomal recessive rate (1/500K to 1/250K), annual US incidence is approximately 4–14 new cases/year. Nearly all new AKU births remain undiagnosed until adulthood because AKU is not on current newborn screening panels in any US state.

Newborn Screening Opportunity

AKU is not included in the US Recommended Uniform Screening Panel (RUSP). HGA is detectable in infant urine via MS/MS methods and inclusion is technically feasible. A RUSP nomination would convert AKU from a decades-delayed diagnosis to a birth-time identification, but would represent a multi-year investment through the Advisory Committee on Heritable Disorders in Newborns and Children.

EHR Identification Opportunities

With claims codes non-specific and the prescriber universe under 50, the practical question is: where are the 200–1,200 undiagnosed US patients, and how do we surface them? EHR data — structured phenotype plus free-text mining across federated networks — is the only data surface with sufficient granularity. Four complementary approaches, in rough order of yield:

1 · Structured Phenotype — Computable Definition

Identifies patients with the “AKU-shaped” comorbidity pattern, filterable against federated EHR networks.

  • Age 30–65 at first OA diagnosis
  • Severe OA in ≥3 joint groups (spine + hip + knee/shoulder) — ICD M15.*, M16.*, M17.*, M47.*
  • Joint replacement before age 55 — CPT 27130, 27447, 23472, 22610
  • Negative autoimmune workup — normal RF, anti-CCP, ANA, HLA-B27
  • Optional enrichment: aortic valve replacement or diagnosis before age 65 (I35.0, I35.2, CPT 33361–33369)
  • Optional enrichment: renal/urinary stones with unusual pigment (N20.*)

Expected yield: a broad candidate pool that still needs enrichment — expect thousands of phenotype-positive patients per 10M covered lives, most of whom will not have AKU. Combine with signal 2 or 3 for specificity.

2 · Clinical-Note NLP — Free-Text Signals

The highest-specificity signals are rarely coded. They live in narrative text: surgical notes, pathology reports, ophthalmology exams. NLP on clinical notes inside the EHR is the fastest path from “phenotype” to “almost certainly AKU.”

  • Surgical / pathology notes: “black cartilage,” “pigmented cartilage,” “ochronotic,” “ochronosis,” “dark cartilage,” “bluish-black discoloration”
  • Urology / PCP notes: “dark urine,” “urine turns black on standing,” “black diaper” (historical)
  • Ophthalmology: “scleral pigmentation,” “pigmented sclera,” “ochronotic pigmentation”
  • Derm / ENT: “bluish ear cartilage,” “blackened cerumen,” “black earwax”
  • Cardiology: “ochronotic valve,” “pigmented aortic valve,” “atypical valve calcification”

Expected yield: very high specificity. A single hit of “ochronotic” or “black cartilage” in a pathology report is close to diagnostic. This is the single most productive EHR lever.

3 · Lab-Result Mining — Latent HGA Signals

Patients who received a metabolic workup but weren’t formally diagnosed leave a trail of orderable tests. EHR lab tables and reference-lab integrations can surface them.

  • Urine organic acid panel (LOINC 34362-5, 25476-5) with HGA elevation noted in free-text interpretation
  • Quantitative urine HGA ordered but never reconciled back into a diagnosis (common in tertiary centers)
  • Ferric-chloride screen positive (rare, but preserved in some pediatric records)
  • Unexplained elevated urine “chromogen” or atypical color on urinalysis automation

Expected yield: low-volume but extremely high-precision. This catches patients already worked-up but not followed through.

4 · Diagnostic-Cascade Detection — Behavioral Patterns

Model the typical journey itself — “tour of specialists without resolution” — as a signal. Most AKU patients visit 3+ specialties over 5+ years without a unifying diagnosis.

  • ≥3 rheumatology visits within 24 months with no inflammatory/autoimmune diagnosis recorded
  • Referral chain: rheumatology → orthopedics → genetics (rare ordering signal)
  • Multiple joint replacements in patient <55 in the same EHR
  • Aortic valve calcification imaging in patient <60 with no CKD, diabetes, or hyperlipidemia
  • Kidney stone analysis report with “dark” or “pigmented” descriptor

Expected yield: behavioral signal — useful as a second filter on the structured phenotype, less useful standalone.

Recommended sequence

  1. Genotype sweep first. Commercial genetic labs already carry HGD biallelic positives from carrier screens and broad panels. De-identified counts by geography give the sharpest picture of diagnosed-but-unreached patients and ground-truth the denominator.
  2. Note-NLP pilot in one large academic partner. Search surgical pathology reports for “ochronotic,” “ochronosis,” “black cartilage.” Expect 0.5–3 true positives per million adult records — extremely small absolute numbers, but almost all are true AKU and most are undiagnosed.
  3. Structured phenotype at scale. Run Signal 1 across Epic Cosmos / TriNetX for denominator sizing. Cross with Signal 4 (behavioral cascade) to rank candidates for outreach.
  4. Disease-awareness intervention, measured. Target rheumatology and orthopedic pathology departments at the top 20 academic medical centers with “consider AKU in early-onset severe OA” education. Measure incremental urine-HGA orders and new AKU diagnoses pre/post.
  5. RUSP nomination (long horizon). Build the cost-effectiveness and treatment-available case for AKU inclusion on newborn screening. Even without success, the effort drives disease awareness.
