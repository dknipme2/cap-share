ORMALVI (Dichlorphenamide) — Periodic Paralysis

ORMALVI (dichlorphenamide) is an oral carbonic anhydrase inhibitor (CAI), approved for the treatment of the primary periodic paralyses (hypokalemic PP, hyperkalemic PP, and related variants including Andersen-Tawil syndrome). Marketed as a branded ANDA generic by Cycle Pharmaceuticals alongside the brand originator Keveyis (Xeris). Orphan exclusivity expired in 2022; the molecule itself has been known since 1958.

Core Insight

This is an ultra-rare channelopathy with a tiny, named prescriber universe and a single FDA-approved molecule. The commercial challenge for ORMALVI is not finding the right prescriber — it's finding the undiagnosed patients sitting inside rheumatology, ER, and general-neurology records. Patients endure years of misdiagnosis (as conversion disorder, psychiatric weakness, or idiopathic myopathy) before a neuromuscular specialist orders the right panel. With LeMed exclusive distribution and the Cycle Vita patient support hub, Cycle Pharmaceuticals has full visibility on every ORMALVI fill — the commercial value is in surfacing the undiagnosed population.

Disease & Epidemiology

The primary periodic paralyses are autosomal dominant skeletal-muscle channelopathies caused by mutations in ion-channel genes: SCN4A (sodium channel, HyperPP and some HypoPP), CACNA1S (calcium channel, ~60% of HypoPP), KCNJ2 (inward-rectifier potassium channel, Andersen-Tawil), and KCNE3. Attacks of flaccid paralysis are triggered by exercise-rest cycles, carbohydrate-rich or potassium-rich meals, cold, stress, or fasting — depending on subtype.

Clinical hallmarks

  • Flaccid paralysis attacks — transient, typically lasting hours; provoked by rest after exercise, diet, or stress
  • Hypokalemic PP: serum K⁺ drops during attack; triggered by carb-rich meals, rest after exertion
  • Hyperkalemic PP: serum K⁺ rises during attack; triggered by potassium-rich foods, fasting, cold
  • Andersen-Tawil: PP + cardiac arrhythmias (long QT, bidirectional VT) + dysmorphic features
  • Permanent weakness can develop between episodic attacks over decades
  • Family history is typically present (autosomal dominant) but de novo mutations occur

Diagnostic lag is the real problem

Patients report years to decades between first attack and correct diagnosis. Misdiagnoses include conversion disorder, psychiatric weakness, hypokalemia of unclear etiology, myasthenia gravis, and idiopathic myopathy. Commercial analytics for this product needs to treat diagnostic-odyssey reduction as the primary lever — the treated population is a fraction of the diagnosable population, which is a fraction of the true prevalent population.

Patient Journey

Age 5–20 · First attack
Episodic weakness — often dismissed or misattributed
First paralytic attack typically occurs in adolescence (HypoPP) or earlier childhood (HyperPP). Triggered by specific meals or post-exercise rest. Many families describe the child as "going limp" for hours. Initial explanations range from dehydration to anxiety to malingering.
ER / urgent care
First medical encounter — serum K+ drawn during attack
Some patients present to the ER mid-attack. Serum potassium is measured and found low (HypoPP) or high (HyperPP). Without family history or a high-index clinician, the event is coded as E87.6 (hypokalemia) or E87.5 (hyperkalemia) and the underlying channelopathy is missed.
Pediatrics / PCP
Recurrent attacks → neurology referral
After multiple episodes, pediatrics or primary care refers to neurology. Delay from first attack to first neurology visit can be months to years depending on geography and caregiver persistence.
General neurology
EMG with exercise provocation — if ordered
A neuromuscular-literate neurologist orders an EMG with exercise provocation — the classic electrodiagnostic test for PP. Without exposure to channelopathies, general neurologists often pursue autoimmune workups (which are uniformly negative) and lose years before referring on.
Neuromuscular subspecialty
Genetic panel + attack diary + diagnosis
At an academic neuromuscular clinic or Center of Excellence, a neuromuscular channelopathy panel (SCN4A, CACNA1S, KCNJ2, KCNE3) is ordered. Family history is re-elicited. Attack diary is kept. Diagnosis is confirmed.
On therapy
Dichlorphenamide trial or acetazolamide (off-label)
First-line pharmacotherapy is dichlorphenamide (FDA-approved) or acetazolamide (off-label, cheaper, ~50% response rate overall, lower in SCN4A). Dichlorphenamide shows Class I evidence in HypoPP (attack rate 0.3 vs. 2.4 per week, p=0.02) and weaker but positive evidence in HyperPP. Potassium supplementation or restriction is added as supportive care depending on subtype.

EHR Identification Opportunities

Unlike alkaptonuria (where no PP-specific ICD exists), ORMALVI-era PP has a specific ICD code — G72.3 — but it is under-used, and the real diagnostic signal lives in attack-context coding and genetic-panel orders. The practical EHR question is: where are the patients miscoded as hypokalemia/hyperkalemia during a paralytic episode, or ordered a neuromuscular channelopathy panel but never reconciled back to a diagnosis?

1 · Structured ICD Signal

PP-specific coding is under-used but present. Combine with episodic-context codes for enrichment.

  • Primary ICD: G72.3 (periodic paralysis)
  • Hypokalemia in paralyzing-episode context: E87.6
  • Hyperkalemia in paralyzing-episode context: E87.5
  • Family history of hereditary channelopathy
  • Optional enrichment: neuromuscular disorder codes (G71.*) co-occurring with potassium abnormalities

Expected yield: moderate specificity. G72.3 alone captures many already-diagnosed patients; combining with episodic potassium coding enriches for undiagnosed cases.

2 · Genetic-Test Order Signals

Neuromuscular channelopathy panel orders (ordered but not reconciled into a diagnosis) are extremely high-specificity leads.

  • Ordered panel: SCN4A, CACNA1S, KCNJ2, KCNE3
  • Multi-gene NM channelopathy panels from major genetics labs
  • Single-gene SCN4A or CACNA1S sequencing orders
  • "Neuromuscular comprehensive" panel orders with no follow-up ICD

Expected yield: very high specificity. Commercial genetic labs already carry the list of tested-positive patients; partnership access is the highest-precision patient-finding lever.

3 · Electrodiagnostic Signals

EMG with exercise-provocation testing is the classic electrodiagnostic workup. Order codes + result narratives are searchable.

  • EMG with long exercise test CPT codes
  • Free-text: "post-exercise CMAP decrement," "long exercise test positive"
  • EMG reports noting "suggestive of channelopathy"
  • Referral patterns from EMG lab back to neuromuscular subspecialty

Expected yield: high specificity in EMG-heavy academic EHRs; less useful in community settings where the test is rarely ordered.

4 · Clinical-Note NLP — Free-Text Signals

The highest-value PP signals live in neurology notes. Short pilot on academic NM clinic notes typically surfaces undiagnosed candidates.

  • Neurology / NM notes: "flaccid paralysis," "periodic paralysis," "episodic weakness with hypokalemia," "channelopathy," "Andersen-Tawil"
  • ER notes: "paralytic episode," "serum K low with weakness," "recurrent hypokalemia with paralysis"
  • Cardiology notes: "long QT with episodic weakness," "bidirectional VT"
  • Pediatrics: "episodic limb weakness," "can't move legs after meals"

Expected yield: very high precision. A single "flaccid paralysis with hypokalemia" hit in neurology notes is close to diagnostic.

5 · Specialty-Cascade Detection

The typical PP patient cycles through ER → general neurology → neuromuscular subspecialty. The cascade itself is signal.

  • Repeated ER visits for "weakness" with potassium abnormalities in patient <40
  • General neurology visit with negative autoimmune workup (normal RF, CCP, ANA) followed by NM referral
  • Referral pattern: ER → neurology → genetics
  • Multiple EMGs without resolution

6 · Family-History Signals

Autosomal dominant inheritance means patients often have a family history. EHR family-history structured fields and narrative can surface kindred with known-PP relatives.

  • Structured family-history PP entries
  • Narrative: "mother with periodic paralysis," "father has episodic weakness"
  • Multiple family members with channelopathy genetic testing

Open Questions

  1. Does LeMed report to IQVIA Xponent / Symphony DDD? Binary question; determines whether World 1 or World 2 analytics apply (see cluster page).
  2. How many patients are actually on ORMALVI vs. the total diagnosed universe? Revenue-implied treated-patient counts suggest ~1,500–2,500 on therapy, vs. <3,000 diagnosed and ~5,000 prevalent.
  3. DAW compliance rate at LeMed — substitution to unbranded generic dichlorphenamide at ~$67/tablet erodes ORMALVI branded-generic economics silently.
  4. Is off-label acetazolamide displacing ORMALVI in cost-sensitive payer environments? No head-to-head trial exists.
  5. What is the shape of the genetic-testing pipeline — how many tested-positive-but-untreated patients exist at the major commercial labs?
  6. Center of Excellence share — does a small number of academic NM clinics account for most diagnoses, and can disease-awareness investment at community neurology expand the funnel?
